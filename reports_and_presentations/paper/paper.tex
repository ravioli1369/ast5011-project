\begin{abstract}
      Stellar streams in the Milky Way halo are highly sensitive probes of the Galactic potential and the small-scale distribution of dark matter (DM). In this study, we characterize the density structure of seven stellar streams (GD-1, Jhelum, Indus, NGC 6397, C-12, Orphan, and Ylgr) to identify density gaps and underdensities potentially induced by DM subhalos. Using Gaia DR3 \citep{Vallenari2023} astrometry and photometry, along with the \texttt{STREAMFINDER} catalog \citep{Ibata2024, Malhan2018} we extract 1D stellar density profiles along the stream tracks.

      The observational results are compared against a theoretical framework derived from the TNG50-1 IllustrisTNG simulation \citep{Nelson2019}. We identify Milky Way like host halos and extract their associated subhalo populations, focusing on the $10^6$--$10^9 M_{\odot}$ mass range most likely to perturb stellar streams. To contrast Cold Dark Matter (CDM) with Warm Dark Matter (WDM) predictions, we model free-streaming suppression by identifying suppressed halo seeds at high redshift ($z \approx 20$) and tracing their evolution to the present day using the \texttt{SubLink} merger tree \citep{Rodriguez2015}. By quantifying the expected frequency and spatial distribution of these subhalos, we evaluate the consistency of observed stream perturbations with CDM and WDM cosmologies.
    \end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\vspace{-0.1cm}
Stellar streams in the Milky Way halo serve as high-precision dynamical tracers, sensitive to perturbations from low-mass dark matter subhalos \citep{Bonaca2025}. This project investigates the density structure of seven streams using Gaia DR3 data \citep{Vallenari2023} and compares these observations to theoretical benchmarks from the TNG50-1 simulation \citep{Nelson2019}.

\section{Data Description}

\subsection{Observational: Gaia DR3 and the Streamfinder Atlas}

The dataset used for the GD-1 stream \citep{Grillmair2006,Price2018} is sourced from the Gaia Data Release 3 (DR3) catalog \citep{Vallenari2023}, which provides high-precision astrometry and photometry for over 1.8 billion stars. For the other streams (Jhelum, Indus, NGC 6397, C-12, Orphan, and Ylgr), we use the Streamfinder Atlas catalog \citep{Malhan2018, Ibata2024}, a specialized dataset of 24,540 stars identified as stream candidates. This catalog provides extinction-corrected G-band magnitudes and (B--R) colors, alongside 5D astrometry.

\subsection{Theoretical: IllustrisTNG}

Theoretical predictions for dark matter substructure are derived from the TNG50-1 run of the IllustrisTNG project, a high-resolution magneto-hydrodynamic cosmological simulation \citep{Nelson2019}. We utilize the $z=0$ Friends-of-Friends (FoF) group catalogs to select Milky Way-mass host halos and the \texttt{Subfind} algorithm to identify their associated satellite subhalos. To model the effects of Warm Dark Matter (WDM), we utilize the \texttt{SubLink} \citep{Rodriguez2015} merger trees to trace the assembly history of these subhalos, identifying those whose "seeds" at high redshift $(z\approx 20)$ would be suppressed by free-streaming effects.



\section{Analysis Methods}
\subsection{Stellar Stream Density Extraction}
For the GD-1 stream, we follow the methodology outlined in \citet{Bonaca2025}, consisting of three primary steps to isolate stream members as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_selection}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coordinate Transformation:} ICRS coordinates are rotated into a stream-aligned frame $(\phi_1, \phi_2)$ using the rotation matrix defined by \texttt{galstreams} \citep{Mateu2023}, such that the stream lies along $\phi_2 \approx 0^{\circ}$.
    \item \textbf{Photometric Matched Filter:} A selection polygon is applied in the color-magnitude diagram (CMD) tracing a PARSEC isochrone tailored to GD-1 ($[M/H] \approx -2.2$, distance $\approx 7.5$ kpc, age $\approx 12$ Gyr).
    \item \textbf{Proper Motion Selection:} Stream members are isolated by selecting the high-density clump in proper motion space $(\mu_{\phi_1}, \mu_{\phi_2})$ aligned with the stream's orbital motion.
\end{enumerate}

In order to obtain the fit of the stream track, we apply a Gaussian smoothing kernel to the 2D spatial distribution. We then identify peaks in $\phi_2$ for each longitudinal bin $\phi_1$. A second-order polynomial is then fit to these points to define an analytic track $\phi_2(\phi_1)$ as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_track}. This track is used to define the selection box for the 1D density profiles.

\begin{figure*}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../plots/GD-1_track.png}
    \caption{Determination of the analytic stream track in the $(\phi_1, \phi_2)$ coordinate system. The background contours represent the smoothed stellar density $S(\phi_1, \phi_2)$. Red markers (\textit{x}) indicate the identified density ridge points, defined as the peaks in $\phi_2$ that exceed the 75th percentile of the global density. The solid black line represents the best-fit quadratic model.}
    \label{fig:gd1_track}
\end{figure*}

1D density profiles are constructed by histogramming the surviving stars along a $0.5^\circ$ wide bin around the track as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_density}. Background subtraction is performed by estimating the background levels from adjacent off-stream control regions.

\subsection{Dark Matter Comparison Framework}
To evaluate different dark matter cosmologies, we compare the observed gap frequencies to predictions from Cold Dark Matter (CDM) and Warm Dark Matter (WDM) scenarios. The CDM baseline is established directly from the TNG50-1 subhalo mass function. To model WDM, we simulate free-streaming suppression by identifying subhalo ``seeds'' at high redshift ($z \approx 20$) and tracing their assembly history to the present day using the \texttt{SubLink} merger trees \citep{Nelson2019}. By removing subhalos that would have been suppressed by WDM free-streaming, we estimate the resulting change in the expected number of stream-perturbing impacts.

\subsubsection*{Cold Dark Matter Modelling}

From the TNG50-1 simulation, we select host halos with virial masses around $10^{12} M_{\odot}$ to represent Milky Way analogs, which leaves us with $\sim 100$ suitable hosts. For each host, we extract the subhalo population and construct the subhalo mass function (SHMF) in the mass range $10^7$--$10^9 M_{\odot}$, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:shmrf_cdm}. Subhalos smaller than $10^7 M_{\odot}$ are not resolved in the simulation, and are not capable of producing observable gaps in stellar streams, while subhalos larger than $10^9 M_{\odot}$ are large enough to host luminous satellites and are not expected to be dark. Figure \ref{fig:shmrf_cdm} also shows the radial distribution of subhalos within the host halos. We select subhalos that are between a radial distance of $10-100$ kpc from the host center, as these are most likely to interact with stellar streams.

We then compute the expected number of impacts on a stream by following the framework outlined in \citet{Erkal2015}. The encounter rate is calculated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:encounter_rate}
    \dfrac{dN}{dt} = \dfrac{2}{V_\text{shell}} v_\text{rel} \int_{M_\text{min}}^{M_\text{max}} b(M) dM,
\end{equation}

where $V_\text{shell}$ is the volume of the shell around the stream, $v_\text{rel}$ is the relative velocity between the stream and subhalos (taken to be $\sim 200$ km/s), and $b(M)$ is the impact parameter for a subhalo of mass $M$ that can produce a detectable gap. Scale radius normalized $b(M)$ is taken to be $5$, and the scale radius is calculated from Equation 15 of \citet{Erkal2016}. The integral is performed over the mass range of interest.

Equation \ref{eq:encounter_rate} provides the expected number of encounters per kpc per Gyr. To obtain the total expected number of encounters per kpc, we multiply this rate by the approximate age of stellar streams, which is around $3$~Gyr. Note that the age of the stars in the streams is closer to $12$~Gyr, but the streams themselves formed around $3$~Gyr ago. This gives us the expected number of encounters per kpc for a given stream under the CDM scenario.
