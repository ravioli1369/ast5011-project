\begin{abstract}
      Stellar streams in the Milky Way halo are highly sensitive probes of the Galactic potential and the small-scale distribution of dark matter (DM). In this study, we characterize the density structure of seven stellar streams (GD-1, Jhelum, Indus, NGC 6397, C-12, Orphan, and Ylgr) to identify density gaps and underdensities potentially induced by DM subhalos. Using Gaia DR3 \citep{Vallenari2023} astrometry and photometry, along with the \texttt{STREAMFINDER} catalog \citep{Ibata2024, Malhan2018} we extract 1D stellar density profiles along the stream tracks.

      The observational results are compared against a theoretical framework for the small-scale distribution of dark matter derived from the TNG50-1 IllustrisTNG simulation \citep{Nelson2019}. We identify Milky Way like host halos and extract their associated subhalo populations, focusing on the $10^6$--$10^9 M_{\odot}$ mass range most likely to perturb stellar streams. To contrast Cold Dark Matter (CDM) with Warm Dark Matter (WDM) predictions, we model free-streaming suppression by identifying and excluding from our analysis any subhalos whose $z=0$ masses correspond to a comoving radius less than the free-streaming scale. By quantifying the expected frequency and spatial distribution of these subhalos, we evaluate the consistency of observed stream perturbations with CDM and WDM cosmologies.
    \end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\vspace{-0.1cm}
Stellar streams in the Milky Way halo serve as high-precision dynamical tracers, sensitive to perturbations from low-mass dark matter subhalos \citep{Bonaca2025}. This project investigates the density structure of seven streams using Gaia DR3 data \citep{Vallenari2023} and compares these observations to theoretical benchmarks from the TNG50-1 simulation \citep{Nelson2019}.

\section{Data Description}

\subsection{Observational: Gaia DR3 and the Streamfinder Atlas}

The dataset used for the GD-1 stream \citep{Grillmair2006,Price2018} is sourced from the Gaia Data Release 3 (DR3) catalog \citep{Vallenari2023}, which provides high-precision astrometry and photometry for over 1.8 billion stars. For the other streams (Jhelum, Indus, NGC 6397, C-12, Orphan, and Ylgr), we use the Streamfinder Atlas catalog \citep{Malhan2018, Ibata2024}, a specialized dataset of 24,540 stars identified as stream candidates. This catalog provides extinction-corrected G-band magnitudes and (B--R) colors, alongside 5D astrometry.

\subsection{Theoretical: IllustrisTNG}

Theoretical predictions for dark matter substructure are derived from the TNG50-1 run of the IllustrisTNG project, a high-resolution magneto-hydrodynamic cosmological simulation \citep{Nelson2019}. We utilize the $z=0$ Friends-of-Friends (FoF) group catalogs to select Milky Way-mass host halos and the \texttt{Subfind} algorithm to identify their associated satellite subhalos. We also use this data to model the predicted subhalo distribution of Warm Dark Matter (WDM) with a particle mass of $1\,\text{keV}$ by discarding halos with mass below the threshold mass that could form a halo in the presence of WDM free-streaming.



\section{Analysis Methods}
\subsection{Stellar Stream Density Extraction}
For the GD-1 stream, we follow the methodology outlined in \citet{Bonaca2025}, consisting of three primary steps to isolate stream members as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_selection}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coordinate Transformation:} ICRS coordinates are rotated into a stream-aligned frame $(\phi_1, \phi_2)$ using the rotation matrix defined by \texttt{galstreams} \citep{Mateu2023}, such that the stream lies along $\phi_2 \approx 0^{\circ}$.
    \item \textbf{Photometric Matched Filter:} A selection polygon is applied in the color-magnitude diagram (CMD) tracing a PARSEC isochrone tailored to GD-1 ($[M/H] \approx -2.2$, distance $\approx 7.5$ kpc, age $\approx 12$ Gyr).
    \item \textbf{Proper Motion Selection:} Stream members are isolated by selecting the high-density clump in proper motion space $(\mu_{\phi_1}, \mu_{\phi_2})$ aligned with the stream's orbital motion.
\end{enumerate}

In order to obtain the fit of the stream track, we apply a Gaussian smoothing kernel to the 2D spatial distribution. We then identify peaks in $\phi_2$ for each longitudinal bin $\phi_1$. A second-order polynomial is then fit to these points to define an analytic track $\phi_2(\phi_1)$ as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_track}. This track is used to define the selection box for the 1D density profiles.

Red markers (\textit{x}) indicate the identified density ridge points, defined as the peaks in $\phi_2$ that exceed the 75th percentile of the global density. The solid black line represents the best-fit quadratic model.}

1D density profiles are constructed by histogramming the surviving stars along a $0.5^\circ$ wide bin around the track as shown in Figure \ref{fig:gd1_density}. Background subtraction is performed by estimating the background levels from adjacent off-stream control regions.

\subsection{Dark Matter Comparison Framework}
To evaluate the observed stream perturbations against the theoretical distribution of dark matter, we compare the observed gap frequencies to predictions from Cold Dark Matter (CDM) and Warm Dark Matter (WDM) scenarios. The CDM baseline is established directly from the TNG50-1 subhalo mass function. The WDM baseline is derived from the same TNG50-1 simulation suite by identifying and removing subhalos that would be suppressed by WDM free-streaming.

\subsubsection*{Cold Dark Matter Modelling}

From the TNG50-1 simulation, we select host halos with virial masses around $10^{12} M_{\odot}$ to represent Milky Way analogs, which leaves us with $\sim 100$ suitable hosts. For each host, we extract the subhalo population and construct the subhalo mass function (SHMF) in the mass range $10^7$--$10^9 M_{\odot}$, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:shmrf_cdm}. Subhalos smaller than $10^7 M_{\odot}$ are not resolved in the simulation, and are not capable of producing observable gaps in stellar streams, while subhalos larger than $10^9 M_{\odot}$ are large enough to host luminous satellites and are not expected to be dark. Figure \ref{fig:shmrf_cdm} also shows the radial distribution of subhalos within the host halos. We select subhalos that are between a radial distance of $10-100$ kpc from the host center, as these are most likely to interact with stellar streams.

We then compute the expected number of impacts on a stream by following the framework outlined in \citet{Erkal2015}. The encounter rate is calculated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:encounter_rate}
    \dfrac{dN}{dt} = \dfrac{2}{V_\text{shell}} v_\text{rel} \int_{M_\text{min}}^{M_\text{max}} b(M) dM,
\end{equation}

where $V_\text{shell}$ is the volume of the shell around the stream, $v_\text{rel}$ is the relative velocity between the stream and subhalos (taken to be $\sim 200$ km/s), and $b(M)$ is the impact parameter for a subhalo of mass $M$ that can produce a detectable gap. Scale radius normalized $b(M)$ is taken to be $5$, and the scale radius is calculated from Equation 15 of \citet{Erkal2016}. The integral is performed over the mass range of interest.

Equation \ref{eq:encounter_rate} provides the expected number of encounters per kpc per Gyr. To obtain the total expected number of encounters per kpc, we multiply this rate by the approximate age of stellar streams, which is around $3$~Gyr. Note that the age of the stars in the streams is closer to $12$~Gyr, but the streams themselves formed around $3$~Gyr ago. This gives us the expected number of encounters per kpc for a given stream under the CDM scenario.

We find the observed perturbation rate of $\sim 0.068$ perturbations per kpc to be slightly higher than the $1\sigma$ upper bound of the expected perturbation rate from the CDM scenario. This suggests that the observed stream perturbations are consistent with, but on the higher end of, CDM predictions, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:sprd_cmd}.

\subsubsection*{Warm Dark Matter Modelling}

Compared to CDM, Warm Dark Matter (WDM) particles remain relativistic for a longer duration in the early universe. This extended period of "free-streaming" smoothes out small-scale density fluctuations in the matter power spectrum, effectively blurring nearby overdensities. The redshift $z_{nr}$ at which WDM particles become non-relativistic is fundamentally linked to their mass $m_{\text{wdm}}$ through the relation:
\begin{equation}
    m_{\text{wdm}}c^2 = k_B T(z_{nr}).
\end{equation}

This transition defines a characteristic free-streaming length, $\lambda_{fs}$, which represents the distance a particle travels before becoming non-relativistic; as a comoving distance, $\lambda_{fs}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{fs} = c t(z_{nr})(1 + z_{nr}).
\end{equation}

For our chosen WDM particle mass of $1\,\text{keV}$, the corresponding free-streaming length is $\lambda_{fs} \approx 54 kpc$.

This distance dictates a minimum scale for the formation of dark matter structures in the early universe. An overdense and underdense region that are within  $\lambda_{fs}$ of each other will get ``evened out'' by WDM free-streaming; only overdensities at and above this scale will survive to coalesce into halos, resulting in a larger starting point for bottom-up formation and the near-complete absence of halos below this scale.

Since direct WDM cosmological simulations were not accessible for this study, we sought to apply the expected effects of WDM free-streaming to the TNG50-1 data used in our CDM analysis. One approach we considered was using the simulation's evolutionary snapshots, which capture provide data for the simulation box at a variety of redshifts, to reconstruct the chain of bottom-up formation that would occur in the presence of WDM. In principle, the \texttt{SubLink} merger tree \citep{Rodriguez2015} allows us to trace a collection of $z=0$ subhalos back to their early-universe progenitors, flag any of those progenitors that would have merged due to free-streaming effects, and then propogate those changes back to the $z=0$ snapshot. In practice, however, we found that the earliest available snapshot (snapshot 0, corresponding to $z\approx 20$) captures data from too late in the universe to perform this analysis; at this point, the overdensities that WDM free-streaming would have suppressed have already moved outside the free-streaming distance due to expansion and have started collapsing into halos.

Instead, we determined which TNG50-1 halos would be suppressed by WDM through a theoretical derivation of the present-day cutoff mass. We take the free-streaming distance $\lambda_{fs}$ to be the radius of a theoretical halo's Lagrangian volume, then compute that halo's corresponding mass using the \texttt{Colossus} cosmology toolkit \citep{Diemer2018}. We then take this mass as the lower bound for the present-day, $z=0$ mass of halos that could form in the presence of WDM, as any smaller halos would have a comoving Lagrangian radius smaller than $\lambda_{fs}$ and thus have been ``smoothed out'' by free-streaming early in their history. 

We applied the same analysis used with the baseline TNG50-1 to this ``mock-WDM'' data, beginning with a recalculation of the subhalo mass function (SHMF). As expected, the resulting SHMF follows the same distribution as for CDM save for being truncated at the mass scale corresponding to $\lambda_{fs}$; for our choice of $m_{\text{wdm}}$, the low-mass cutoff is $M_\text{subhalo}=1.3\times10^8\,M_\odot$.

While the radial distribution of the remaining subhalos maintains a similar shape to the CDM scenario (as seen in Figure \ref{fig:shmrf_wdm}), the overall number density of subhalos is significantly reduced. Consequently, the expected number of stream-perturbing encounters is much lower in the WDM model than in the CDM model. 

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:sprd_wdm}, this results in a predicted perturbation rate that is significantly lower than our observed rate of $\sim 0.068$ perturbations per kpc. The fact that the observed rate sits well outside the expected distribution for this WDM model suggests that the observed stream perturbations are more consistent with CDM predictions, potentially allowing us to place lower bounds on the allowable mass of WDM particles.
